03/19/2019 #9 ORDER of TRANSFER: In Barbosa v. Commonwealth (No. 2), 481 Mass. 1016 (2018), we ordered that this appeal be entered directly in this court. We did so, as we explained, on the understanding that the defendant had been convicted of being an habitual offender pursuant to G. L. c. 279, § 25 (b), and with the expectation that the case would give us an opportunity to decide an important, as yet unresolved jurisdictional question, i.e., whether direct appeals from convictions under § 25 (b) must be entered in, and decided by, this court in the first instance or, instead, should be entered in the Appeals Court. See id. at 1017-1018. After the case was entered here, however, the defendant's counsel[1] informed us that the defendant was not in fact convicted under G. L. c. 279, § 25 (b), but rather was convicted under § 25 (a) of c. 279. There is no question that direct appeals from convictions under § 25 (a) must be entered in the Appeals Court in the first instance. Because a conviction under § 25 (a) does not give rise to the same jurisdictional question as a conviction under § 25 (b), this case will not give us the opportunity to consider the question after all. It is therefore ordered that this appeal be transferred to the Appeals Court, where it should have been entered in the first place. The defendant, through counsel, argues that the appeal nevertheless should remain in this court because the underlying legal issues merit this court's attention. The Commonwealth opposes that request. We agree with the Commonwealth. If the defendant wishes to press his argument that the appeal ought to be decided by this court in the first instance, he is free to file an application for direct appellate review, which we will consider in due course. By the Court

[1] The defendant was not represented by counsel in Barbosa v. Commonwealth (No. 2), 481 Mass. 1016 (2018). He represented himself.
