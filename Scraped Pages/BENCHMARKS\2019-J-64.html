03/01/2019 #9 MEMORANDUM AND ORDER: This is a summary process action in which the plaintiff seeks to evict the defendant post-foreclosure. After a judge of the Housing Court entered summary judgment in favor of the plaintiff, the defendant filed a timely appeal. The plaintiff moved to set the amount of the appeal bond, and before the amount of that bond was set, the defendant moved to waive the bond requirement based on indigency. See G. L. c. 239, §§ 5, 6. A Housing Court refused to entertain the motion to waive because she deemed it untimely (presumably because it was not filed within ten days of the underlying judgment). Before me now, as single justice, is the defendant's appeal of the setting of the bond. For the reasons that follow, I affirm.

The plaintiff argues, based on the language of G. L. c. 239, § 6, that someone facing eviction pursuant to that section (as opposed to a tenant being evicted pursuant to G. L. c. 239, § 5) has no right to seek a waiver of the appeal bond. I disagree. See Home Sav. Bank of Am. FSB v. Camillo, 45 Mass. App. Ct. 910, 911 (1998) ("Section 6 of c. 239 spells out what damages a bond shall protect in the case of a summary process action arising out of mortgage foreclosure. It does not cause the procedures of § 5 to be inapplicable in such cases"). I also disagree with the plaintiff's argument that the defendant necessarily waived her right to seek a waiver of the appeal bond by not filing a motion to that effect within ten days of the underlying judgment[1]. Finally, I note that the defendant's indigency has been established.

Where the defendant's appeal founders is with regard to the need to show a "not frivolous" appellate issue. G. L. c. 239, § 5. The heart of the judge's ruling is as follows:

"[T]he Plaintiff has proven its prima facie case through the submission of undisputed, admissible documents demonstrating its superior right to possession. The burden shifts to the Defendants to demonstrate, through the use of evidence that would be admissible at trial, specific facts showing that there exists a genuine issue for trial. Here, the Defendants failed to provide admissible evidence, in any form, of specific facts showing that there exists a genuine issue for trial."

The defendant focuses on two potential appellate arguments. First, she argues that one affidavit regarding entry onto the property is deficient as not based on personal knowledge. As the plaintiff points out, such a deficiency -- if it exists -- has no bearing on the case before us, because this was a foreclosure based on a statutory power of sale, not one based on foreclosure by entry[2]. Second, the defendant claims, as she has throughout this matter, that the plaintiff has no standing to bring this summary process action, because it has not registered to do business in Massachusetts pursuant G. L. c. 156D, § 15.03(a). It does not appear that this section would even apply to the plaintiff, and, in any event, the defendant has not shown how noncompliance with the requirement that a corporation file a § 15.03 certificate would render a foreclosure undertaken by such an entity void. Although the "not frivolous" standard establishes a relatively low bar, the plaintiff has not in my view crossed that bar here[3].

The only remaining issue has to do with the amount of the bond. The judge set the bond at less than half of the amount of the outstanding post-foreclosure mortgage payments. I see no legal basis for reducing it further. The "use and occupancy" was set at $866 per month, apparently based on the amount of the monthly mortgage payments due. No evidence of fair market "rent" was provided to the Housing Court judge, or to me. Although my review regarding the amount is de novo, I cannot say that the amount set is excessive. Accordingly, I am constrained to affirm the setting of the appeal bond, as set forth in the Notice of Required appeal bond, dated January 29, 2019. (Milkey, J.) *Notice/Attest/Salvidio, J.

Footnotes

1. Although the relevant statutory text is not as clear as the plaintiff claims, it appears correct that those seeking to have an appeal bond waived are required to file such a motion within ten days of the underlying judgment. I therefore agree with the Housing Court judge that the defendant's motion was untimely. However, the key question is not whether the motion was timely, but instead what legal consequences flow from its untimeliness. The judge appears to have assumed that this deprived her of jurisdiction to consider a waiver of the appeal bond, no matter how indigent the defendants may be, and no matter how meritorious their appellate issues may be. To be sure, when the Legislature has set a deadline for the filing of an appeal, that deadline generally is considered jurisdictional. See Friedman v. Bd. of Registration in Medicine, 414 Mass. 663, 665 (1993). However, the defendant here filed a timely appeal notwithstanding that she had only ten days to do so; her tardiness went only to a subsidiary matter, a motion to waive the posting of an appeal bond that had not yet been set. Absent explicit language regarding the jurisdictional consequences of such tardiness, I find it difficult to believe that the Legislature intended to deprive an indigent party being evicted from being able to prosecute a potentially meritorious argument on appeal. Cf. United States v. Kwai Fun Wong, 135 S. Ct. 1625, 1631-1632 (2015) (enactment of statute of limitations does not bar judicial consideration of equitable tolling absent clear statement by Congress). As the U.S. Supreme Court has stated with respect to Federal statutes, "in applying that clear statement rule, we have made plain that most time bars are nonjurisdictional ... Time and again, we have described filing deadlines as 'quintessential claim-processing rules,' which 'seek to promote the orderly progress of litigation,' but do not deprive a court of authority to hear a case." Id. 1632 (internal citations omitted).

2. I have assumed arguendo that the defendant preserved this issue by timely raising it in the Housing Court.

3. In her written submittal to me, the defendant relied in part on an amicus brief that had been submitted in a case previously before a panel of this court. I note that in that case, we rejected the borrowers' effort to block the foreclosure, concluding that the appeal was "unmeritorious." Ressler v. Deutsche Bank Trust Co. Americas, 92 Mass. App. Ct. 502, 503, 506-509 (2017).
