08/10/2018 #5 ORDER: The husband has filed a petition pursuant to G. L. c. 231, § 118 (first para.) seeking leave to pursue an interlocutory appeal from an order issued by a justice of the Probate and Family Court in on-going contempt proceedings. The husband also requests a stay of that order and the underlying modification
judgment pending his appeal.

The husband's petition assumes that the contempt order is not appealable by right. Where the Probate and Family Court judge has not finally adjudicated the husband in contempt, the order does appear to be interlocutory. However, an interlocutory contempt order that contains an order of commitment may be appealable by right. See Cabral's Case, 18
Mass. App. Ct. 141, 143-144 (1984).

Assuming, as the husband does, without deciding that leave is required, the request for leave to take an interlocutory appeal from the Probate and Family Court judge's order is denied. "The Massachusetts policy disfavoring . . . premature 2 appeals . . . has been vigorously pronounced." Long v. Wickett, 50 Mass. App. Ct. 380, 388 (2000). To overcome the
appellate court's traditional abhorrence of piecemeal appellate review the party seeking interlocutory appellate review must demonstrate unusual and compelling circumstances. Id. at 389.

In his petition, the husband fails to demonstrate unusual or compelling circumstances warranting interlocutory appellate review. The husband's claim that the Probate and Family judge should have credited his evidence pertaining to his finances over his wife's evidence is a common claim raised by numerous litigants appearing before the Probate and Family
Court. Moreover, a claim that the lower court judge's factual findings based on those credibility determinations were in error is not a compelling claim simply for the fact that it is unlikely to be successful. See Johnston v. Johnston, 38 Mass. App. Ct. 531, 536 (1995) ("[T]he credibility of a party or other witness . . . is quintessentially the domain of the trial judge, in which the judge's assessment is close to immune from reversal on appeal except on the most compelling of showings.") Therefore, the husband's request for leave to file an interlocutory appeal is denied.

In his petition, the husband also requests an order staying not just the contempt order but also provisions of the underlying judgment on the wife's complaint for 3 modification. To the extent those requests are properly before me, they are denied. To obtain a stay pending appeal, the husband must demonstrate, inter alia, that his appeal has a likelihood of success on the merits. See C.E. v. J.E., 472 Mass. 1016, 1017 (2015). As stated above, the husband's challenge to the trial judge's factual findings in both the contempt proceedings and the modification are unlikely to result in reversal [1]. See Johnston v. Johnston, supra. For these reasons, the husband's petition is DENIED. (McDonough, J.). *Notice/attest/McSweeny, J.

Footnotes

[1] In so holding, I do not prejudge the merits of the husband's appeals from the modification judgment or the eventual contempt judgment. I hold only that he has not made the required showing at this juncture to demonstrate that he was entitled to a stay pending appeal. The husband remains free to raise the same arguments on a fully developed record in his appeals to a panel of this Court, who will consider his arguments with an open mind.
