09/06/2019 #15 ORDER: Following a three-day trial, a Probate and Family Court judge entered a judgment of divorce nisi dissolving the parties' marriage. The plaintiff argues that the trial judge abused his discretion by ordering that the defendant have physical custody of the parties' minor child, limiting the plaintiff's parenting time, and failing to eliminate the plaintiff's child support obligations. We do not reach the merits of the appeal, however, based on our conclusion that, given the plaintiff's failure to file a new notice of appeal after the trial judge ruled on the plaintiff's motion to amend the judgment, the appeal is not properly before us. See Mass. R. A. P. 4 (a) (3), as appearing in 481 Mass. 1607 (2019).

Discussion. In his judgment of divorce nisi, which the trial judge followed with a memorandum of decision explaining his reasoning, the trial judge granted physical custody of the parties' minor child to the defendant, established a schedule of the parties' parenting time with the child, and ordered the
plaintiff to make weekly child support payments. The plaintiff argues that the trial judge abused his discretion in deviating, without adequate explanation, from the custody and parenting time recommendations made by the court-appointed guardian ad litem (GAL),[1] and, consequently, in ordering the plaintiff to pay child support in excess of that which would have been required if the trial judge had followed the GAL's recommendations.

The judgment entered on August 1, 2017.[2] On August 4, 2017, the plaintiff moved to amend the judgment pursuant to Mass. R. Dom. Rel. P. 59 (e). On August 24, 2017, with his motion to amend still pending, the plaintiff filed a notice of appeal of the judgment. The plaintiff did not renew his notice of appeal following the denial of his motion to amend the judgment on August 30, 2017.

In relevant part, Mass. R. A. P. 4 (a) (3) provides that "[a] notice of appeal filed before the disposition of any timely [motion to alter or amend a judgment under rule 59] shall have no effect."[3] See Eyster v. Pechenik, 71 Mass. App. Ct. 773, 779 (2008) ("It is well settled that a timely filed motion to amend the judgment operates to toll the appeal period, and a notice of appeal filed between the entry of the judgment and the disposition of the motion to amend is a nullity"). In order to preserve the right to appeal, "[a] new notice of appeal must be filed within the prescribed time measured from the entry of the order disposing of the . . . motion." Mass. R. A. P. 4 (a) (3). Here, the plaintiff's sole notice of appeal predated the entry of the trial judge's denial of the plaintiff's motion to amend the judgment; on the entry of that order, the notice was rendered ineffective. In the absence of an effective notice, we are constrained to dismiss the appeal. See DeLucia v. Kfoury, 93 Mass. App. Ct. 166, 170 (2018) (timely notice of appeal is jurisdictional prerequisite to appeal). (Green, C.J., Maldonado, Hand, JJ.[4]). *Notice/Attest
