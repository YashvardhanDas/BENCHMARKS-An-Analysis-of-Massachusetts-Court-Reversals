09/28/2018 #8 ORDER: Antonio Garcia appeals from a judgment of the county court denying his petition for relief under G. L. c. 211, § 3. Garcia has been indicted for trafficking of a person for sexual services, deriving support from prostitution, and, most recently, witness intimidation. On the Commonwealth's motion, and after a dangerousness hearing in the witness intimidation case, a judge in the Superior Court ordered that Garcia be held without bail for a period of not more than 120 days pursuant to G. L. c. 276, § 58A. At the same time, the judge set bail in the sex trafficking cases at $20,000 each, a reduction of amounts that had been set previously. Some time later, Garcia sought a bail hearing, but the Commonwealth objected on the ground that 120 days had not yet run on his § 58A detention. No bail hearing was held at that time. Garcia subsequently filed his G. L. c. 211, § 3, petition, arguing that the judge wrongly denied him release on his personal recognizance.[1] The single justice denied relief without a hearing. The case is before us pursuant to S.J.C. Rule 2:21, as amended, 434 Mass. 1301 (2001), which requires a petitioner seeking relief from an interlocutory ruling of the trial court to "set forth the reasons why review of the trial court decision cannot adequately be obtained on appeal from any final adverse judgment in the trial court or by other available means." Garcia's pro se memorandum does not address this issue, but challenges the § 58A order on the merits and also challenges certain aspects of the grand jury proceedings. Nonetheless, as the Commonwealth acknowledged in its opposition to Garcia's petition, filing a petition pursuant to G. L. c. 211, § 3 is the proper means for seeking relief from bail determinations or pretrial detentions based on dangerousness. See Brangan v. Commonwealth, 477 Mass. 691, 697 (2017). The limited purpose of Rule 2:21 has thus been satisfied. Garcia has no other means of obtaining adequate appellate review of his detention under § 58A. As to Garcia's claims about the grand jury proceedings, however, we see no reason Garcia could not obtain adequate review in the ordinary appellate process after a conviction of any offense. In these circumstances, we allow this appeal from the single justice's judgment to proceed, limited to issues concerning bail and pretrial detention.[2] In its opposition to Garcia's petition, the Commonwealth stated that the trial is scheduled for December 10, 2018. Accordingly, unless the trial is postponed, this case shall be argued in November, 2018, and the parties shall cooperate to establish an expedited briefing schedule. We express no view on the merits of Garcia's claims at this preliminary juncture. By the Court, Francis V. Kenneally, Clerk [1] We note that although Garcia is represented by counsel in the Superior Court, he has proceeded pro se before the single justice and before the full court. [2] We also note that although Garcia has filed a memorandum pursuant to Rule 2:21, he has not filed an appendix presenting the record before the single justice, which is also required by the rule. Ordinarily, this would present a reason not to disturb the judgment of the single justice. See, e.g., Bishay v. Land Ct. Dep't of the Trial Ct., 477 Mass. 1032, 1033 n.2 (2017). However, because it is well established that a petition under G. L. c. 211, § 3, is the appropriate method to obtain review of a bail determination or a pretrial detention order based on dangerousness, we allow the appeal to proceed as to that limited issue.
