02/28/2019 #8 MEMORANDUM AND ORDER: The plaintiff in the underlying action is the record owner of residential property in Worcester that it acquired post-foreclosure. In August of 2018, it brought a summary process action in Housing Court case against the occupants of the property, one of whom was the mortgagor on the loan that was foreclosed. After a bench trial, a judge entered a judgment of possession for the plaintiff. The defendants filed a notice of appeal within ten days of entry of the judgment. However, they also at the same time filed a motion to vacate the judgment. After the motion to vacate the judgment was denied, the defendants did not file a second notice of appeal within ten days thereafter. On this basis, the plaintiff moved to dismiss the defendants' respective appeals. After the judge allowed that motion, the defendants filed a timely appeal of the order dismissing the original notice of appeal.

Meanwhile, the plaintiff obtained an execution of the judgment and proceeded to levy on that execution. Apparently after receiving the so-called "48 hour notice," the defendants filed a motion to stay the eviction, which the Housing Court judge denied. The morning of the scheduled eviction, the defendants filed an emergency motion for a stay with me, as single justice. I stayed the scheduled eviction in order that I could review the matter before it became moot, and I invited further filings from the parties. Having considered the parties' submittals, I continue the stay pending resolution of the defendants' pending appeal. I also sua sponte expedite that appeal.

"An appellant seeking a stay pending appeal must ordinarily meet four tests: (1) the likelihood of appellant's success on the merits; (2) the likelihood of irreparable harm to the appellant if the court denies the stay; (3) the absence of substantial harm to other parties if the stay issues; and (4) the absence of harm to the public interest from granting the stay." C.E. v. J.E., 472 Mass. 1016, 1017 (2015), quoting J.W. Smith & H.P. Zobel, Rules Practice § 62.3, at 409 (2d ed. 2007). As in the analogous context of a request for a preliminary injunction, a particularly strong showing on one element may make up for less strong showing on others. See Packaging Industries Group, Inc. v. Cheney, 380 Mass. 609, 617 n.12, (1980) (if moving party can "demonstrate both that the requested relief is necessary to prevent irreparable harm to it and that granting the injunction poses no substantial risk of such harm to the opposing party, a substantial possibility of success on the merits [rather than a likelihood of success on the merits] warrants issuing the injunction").

Likelihood of success on the merits. The only appeal currently pending is of the order dismissing the original notice of appeal as ineffective. Certainly, as the plaintiff points out, there is case law to support the proposition that "an appeal founded on a notice of appeal filed prior to disposition of a postjudgment motion [that tolls the appeal period] is a nullity and shall be dismissed." Sawyer v. Sawyer, 66 Mass. App. Ct. 906, 907 (2006)."[2] In addition, the principle on which the defendants' original appeal was dismissed finds support in the express language of Mass. R. App. P. 4(a), which states that "[a] notice of appeal filed before the disposition of [motions to modify a judgment] shall have no effect."[3] Nevertheless, I do not see these obstacles necessarily as immutable. For example, at least some argument can be made that where a party otherwise has filed a timely appeal, she could -- in appropriate circumstances -- be relieved of the effects of Rule 4(a) with regard to her having filed a timely notice to vacate the judgment. See Mass. R. App. P. 2 (generally allowing the suspension of the rules "for good cause shown"). Because I believe the present circumstances present such an occasion, I grant such relief to the extent it is within my power to do so.

The summary process statute requires that appeals be filed within a mere ten days of the judgment of possession. G. L. c. 239, § 5.[4] Notwithstanding that the defendants -- like so many other tenants facing eviction -- were representing themselves, they met this statutory deadline when they filed their original notice of appeal. What they apparently failed to appreciate was the counterintuitive effect that their motion to vacate the judgment had on their original -- otherwise timely -- notice of appeal. That is hardly surprising given that this specific jurisdictional snare has been known to trap even seasoned appellate practitioners. Put differently, in my view, this particular quirk of appellate procedure is not one that lay people reasonably can be charged with knowing.

I am, of course, cognizant of the principle that self-represented parties are held to the same procedural rules as those represented by counsel. See, e.g., McCormick v. Labor Relations Comm'n, 412 Mass. 164, 170 n.11 (1992). However, the Supreme Judicial Court has suggested that this principle should bend where the circumstances are such that an unrepresented party thereby would be deprived of an adequate chance to present its arguments in a matter of grave importance. See Carter v. Lynn Housing Authority, 450 Mass. 626, 637 n.17 (2008) (indicating that although a tenant bears the burden of proving mitigating circumstances related to loss of housing subsidy, hearing examiner should take affirmative measures to solicit relevant information). Finally, I note that the Supreme Judicial Court recently has issued an opinion that can be read as calling into question whether the filing of a timely motion to vacate a judgment necessarily renders a notice of appeal of that judgment that was filed before the motion was decided a nullity. See Roch v. Mollica, 481 Mass. 164, 165 n.2 (2019). [5]

The balance of the equities. The stakes to the defendants from the lack of a stay are obvious and strong. The plaintiff has not argued, much less documented, the harm to it in the interim. Of course, common sense dictates that that the plaintiff will suffer some economic harm while the appeal is pending. Any such harm partially can be mitigated through expediting this appeal.

Disposition. Although there certainly is case law to support the judge's order, I believe the defendants nevertheless have a reasonable argument that such cases can and should be revisited. In my view, in light of the unbalanced state of the equities, the defendants have made a sufficient showing regarding their likelihood of success to justify the issuance of stay pending appeal. This also will allow an important issue affecting the administration of justice to be addressed.

For all of these reasons, the existing stay of the defendants' eviction is continued pending resolution of the defendants' appeal noticed on February 1, 2019 following the allowance of plaintiff's motion to dismiss the request for appeal and issuance of the execution. That appeal hereby is ordered to be expedited. The entry fee of the defendants-appellants hereby is waived, and the appeal is entered this date as docket number 2019-P-315. The Clerk of the Eastern District of the Housing Court is to prepare and transmit forthwith notice of assembly of the record, referencing docket number 2019-P-315. The defendants-appellants shall file their brief and appendix[6] on or before April 1, 2019, and the plaintiff-appellee's brief shall be filed on or before May 1, 2019. No party shall seek an enlargement of time to file a brief, except for extraordinary circumstances. The appeal shall be scheduled for argument in June 2019. (Milkey, J.) *Notice/Attest/Horan, J./Salvidio, J.
