07/29/2019 #10 ORDER: This matter came before the Court, Blake, J., presiding on a request from the pro se defendant-appellant for disability accommodations. At this hearing counsel for the plaintiff-appellee and Mr. Saipe appeared. Prior to the hearing, the defendant submitted, among other things, documents from his medical providers. Upon consideration thereof, the court hereby makes the following findings and orders:

1. I find that the defendant's disabilities render him less able to present his arguments in the morning. Therefore I find and order as a reasonable accommodation, that all future hearings shall be scheduled in the afternoon, either at 12:00 P.M. or 2:00 P.M.

2. I find that the defendant's disabilities render him less able to succinctly make arguments orally. As a reasonable accommodation, I order that at any such hearing, each side shall be allotted thirty (30) minutes of argument. This accommodation represents a doubling of the time set forth in Mass. R. App. P. 22 (b). In addition, neither side is permitted rebuttal time.

3. I find that the defendant's disabilities render him less able to remain focused in the courtroom when distracted. Therefore I find and order, that as a reasonable accommodation, the Court shall take all reasonable steps to minimize distractions during any hearing, including but not limited to scheduling the case to be the only case in the courtroom at the time of the hearing.

4. Regarding the defendant-appellant's request to present his case orally, counsel for the plaintiff-appellee has objected and contends that she cannot file a responsive pleading if the case is presented orally, without any briefs. It is the defendant-appellant's responsibility, as the appellant, to identify and brief the issues. The record below demonstrates that the defendant-appellant has presented voluminous pleadings that were either hand written or typed. An appeal requires the filing of a brief and record appendix. See generally Mass. R. App. P. To permit an appeal to proceed without a written brief would fundamentally alter the nature of this court's activities. See Adjartey v. Central Division of the Housing Court Department, 481 Mass. 830, 848-849 (2019). I find that the defendant's disabilities render him less able, though not unable to present written materials to the court; I further find that allowing him to only present his substantive appellate argument orally is contrary to the business practices of this Court. To that end, the following accommodations shall be made. The defendant-appellant may file a brief and appendix in one of three ways. He may file a handwritten brief. He may file a typed brief. He may also file a brief using a dictation or talk-to-text service. This would enable him to dictate his brief and thereafter, print his brief. Regardless of the avenue he chooses, the brief shall comply with Mass. R. App. P.16. It shall include a table of authorities, statement of the issues, statement of the case, statement of the facts and legal argument that cites to the record and to the relevant legal authorities. The brief shall be limited to fifty pages per Mass. R. App. P. 20, however, the Court waives all additional requirements under this rule as to format and pagination. See Mass. R. App. P. (20)(4).

5. In view of the foregoing, I find and order, that as a reasonable accommodation, the original due date for the appellant's brief is extended for the second time until October 4, 2019. No further enlargements shall be anticipated. (Blake, J.). *Notice.

10/22/2019 #16 ORDER: This is an appeal of a summary process action entered by the Metro South Division of the Housing Court Department. The defendant, a tenant whose lease was not renewed, appealed from a judgment for the plaintiff.
On June 11, 2019, the tenant entered his appeal on our docket pursuant to Mass. R.A.P. 10 (a). The same day, he filed a motion seeking a stay and a request for a hearing on disability accommodations. No action was required on the motion to stay, because the issuance of the execution was stayed by operation of law pursuant to Mass. R. Civ. P. 62 and Uniform Summary Process Rule 13.
On July 25, 2019, I conducted a hearing on the tenant's request for disability accommodations. Following the hearing, I issued written findings and orders including, among other things, enlarging the time for filing of the tenant's brief and appendix to October 4, 2019. Approximately one week before the tenant's brief and appendix were due, he filed a motion to reconsider my previous order and a motion for a second hearing on disability accommodation. I allowed the motion for a second hearing on disability accommodations. The scheduling order addressed some issues raised in the tenant's motion to reconsider. Specifically, the hearing was scheduled for 2:30 P.M.; the courtroom was made available at 2:00 P.M.to allow the tenant to set up and get organized; and, contrary to customary practice, the parties were allotted fifteen minutes for rebuttal.
Discussion. The tenant's substantive rights under article 114 of the Amendments to the Massachusetts Constitution, the Massachusetts Equal Rights Act (MERA) [1], and the Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA) [2] are significant. See McDonough, petitioner, 457 Mass. 512, 519-520 (2010). Together, the Massachusetts Constitution, MERA and the ADA entitle the tenant to reasonable accommodations for his disabilities to enable him to participate equally in the services, programs, and activities of the Appeals Court. Adjartey v. Central Division of the Housing Court Department, 481 Mass. 830, 848-849 (2019). "What accommodation is reasonable must be determined on an individualized basis depending on the particular circumstances presented." Id. at 849. It is the tenant's responsibility to identify the reasonable accommodation that he seeks. McDonough, 457 Mass. at 523. However, the judge determines whether the requested accommodation is reasonable, taking into account the totality of the circumstances. Adjartey 481 Mass. at 848-849. Primary consideration must be granted to the requested accommodation; however, a request is unreasonable if it requires the court "to take any action that would fundamentally alter the nature of its services, programs, or activities, or result in an undue financial or administrative burden." Massachusetts Court System, ADA Accessibility Policy (2018); McDonough, 457 Mass at 425.
Findings and orders. I incorporate, by reference, my previous findings regarding the tenant's disabilities and their effect on his ability to prosecute his appeal. a. Timing of hearings. Contrary to his prior request, the tenant has now requested that hearings be scheduled for 2:00 P.M. or later. That accommodation is allowed.
b. Time allotted for hearings. The tenant requested that (1) he be granted more than thirty minutes to present his case at any future hearings and (2) he be permitted time for rebuttal.[3] I decline to enter such an order at this time. The appropriate, reasonable duration of any particular hearing will be decided on a hearing by hearing basis. This order should not be construed as a denial or approval of additional time at any future hearing.
c. Conversational setting. The tenant requests that any further hearings be in a closed "conversational setting" rather than an "adversarial setting." I construe this to be a request that further hearings occur outside the courtroom. Appellate proceedings are open to the public; various members of the court staff must be present; and all hearings must be recorded. Conducting a hearing outside the courtroom would fundamentally alter the nature of the Appeals Court's services, programs, and activities. I find this request to be an unreasonable accommodation.
d. Hearings closed to the public or otherwise limited. The tenant requests that hearings be closed to the public or attendance be limited. Court hearings are open to the public and should be available absent compelling circumstances. See generally Boston Globe Media Partners, LLC v. Chief Justice of the Trial Court, 483 Mass. 80 (2019). Here, the tenant's request for a closed hearing does not sufficiently outweigh the public interest in judicial business being conducted under public scrutiny. Therefore, I decline to enter an order closing any future hearings to the public or otherwise limiting attendance of others. The issue may be raised on a hearing by hearing basis.
e. Hybrid appellate argument. The tenant has requested that he be permitted to present his argument in a hybrid form, by filing a brief and then being guaranteed oral argument is scheduled. The plaintiff objects to this requested accommodation. I construe this to be a request to file an incomplete brief, to be augmented by oral argument.[4]
An appellant is required to file a brief that sets forth his appellate arguments, supported by legal authority. Mass. R. A. P. 16. Failure to include an argument in the written brief constitutes a waiver of the argument, and failure to raise a sufficient appellate argument constitutes waiver of the right to appellate review. Tobin v. Comm'r of Banks, 377 Mass. 909, 909 (1979).
I find that permitting the tenant to present his argument in the hybrid form described above would fundamentally alter the nature of the Appeals Court's services, programs, and activities. I also find that (1) the requested accommodation would unfairly prejudice the plaintiff and (2) the requested accommodation would likely be futile. [5] Therefore, I deny this request. The decision of whether to schedule this case will be made in the ordinary course following the filing of both parties' briefs.
f. Additional time to file brief and record appendix. The tenant has again requested additional time to file his brief and appendix. He requests a due date of January 29, 2020. The plaintiff objects to further enlargements of time. The tenant's request, is allowed in part. His brief and appendix, which were originally due July 21, 2019 are now due on or before November 18, 2019. No further enlargements shall be granted.
I find that there is no apparent connection between the tenant's disabilities and the specific date that he requested. Nevertheless, I do find that some additional time is required as an accommodation. I therefore enlarge the time to file the defendant's brief and appendix to November 18, 2019, which is 120 days after the date on which his brief was originally due.
I note that this is an appeal from a summary process judgment, and the plaintiff's entitlement to the timely recovery of its real property must be balanced against the tenant's right to reasonable accommodations and, generally, to due process. The tenant's written filings, oral presentations, and medical documentation fail to establish a connection between his disabilities and the proposed January 2020 brief due date. Therefore, notwithstanding primary consideration being granted to the requested accommodation, I cannot conclude that the defendant's interests so significantly outweigh the plaintiff's so as to permit the defendant to avoid filing his brief until almost one year after the judgment for the plaintiff entered.[6]
g. Additional requests. Based on his communications to date with the ADA coordinator and to the Clerk's office, the tenant has requested to present his procedural requests to me through staff. This is impermissible. Any communication regarding the substance of his case or procedural matters that affect the parties intended for me or any other judges cannot be made ex parte. Code of Judicial Conduct, Supreme Judicial Court Rule 3:09, Canon 2.9. Similarly, court staff cannot advocate on any party's behalf, and they cannot prepare written court filings on any party's behalf. [7] See generally, Code of Professional Responsibility for Clerks of the Courts, Supreme Court Rule 3:12, Canon 4.[8] No member of the court staff shall spend a disproportionate amount of his and her works hours assisting the tenant in an effort to eliminate any undue administrative burden.
h. Additional materials. The tenant has been directed to the court website for materials to assist him with this appeal. The clerk's office shall print out and mail to the tenant the following materials which are available on the internet: Pro Se Guide to the Appeals Court, template of an appellate brief and appendix, Rules of Appellate Procedure. (Blake, J.). *Notice

11/05/2019 RE#17: The defendant has filed a motion seeking to enlarge the time to file his brief and appendix to February 5, 2020. The basis for the defendant's request are his medical issues.

The defendant has appealed a final judgment for the plaintiff entered by the Housing Court on February 15, 2019 and from the denial of his post-judgment motion to vacate that judgment. The defendant's appeal was entered on this court's docket on June 11, 2019. Pursuant to Mass. R. A. P. 19 (a) (1), his brief and appendix were thus due on or before July 22, 2019 (July 21, 2019 was a Sunday). On June 13, 2019, the defendant filed, inter alia, a request for a hearing on the disability accommodations that he would require. His request for a hearing was allowed, and I ordered his brief to be due on or before August 26, 2019 to allow time for the accommodations hearing to occur and for the implementation of any accommodations that were granted. I held the first accommodations hearing on July 25, 2019. The defendant and counsel for the plaintiff were present at the hearing. On July 29, 2019, I issued my first orders regarding accommodations. I adopt the findings made therein by reference in this order. My July 29, 2019 order extended the time for filing the defendant's brief and appendix to October 4, 2019 as a reasonable accommodation but cautioned the defendant about anticipating further enlargements.

On September 26, 2019, the defendant filed a motion seeking a second hearing on disability accommodations. I allowed his request and conducted a second hearing on October 2, 2019. At that hearing, the defendant requested that his brief and appendix be due by January 29, 2020. On October 22, 2019, I again issued finding and orders relative to disability accommodations, and notwithstanding my previous caution, partially granted the defendant's request for an additional enlargement of time. I ordered him to file his brief and appendix on or before November 18, 2019. I incorporate my findings in that order by reference. In that order, I stated unequivocally that no further enlargements shall be granted.

The within motion is seeking reconsideration of the prohibition on further enlargements, and the defendant is now seeking an enlargement to February 5, 2020, i.e., a week longer that then the date he requested at the October 2, 2019 hearing. In support of his motion, the defendant has filed additional documentation from his medical providers. The additional medical records shall be impounded pending further order of this court or a single justice thereof.

I find that the medical documentation is cumulative of the medical documentation previously submitted and the assertions made by the defendant at the two hearings. Consequently, there is no need for a third hearing on the defendant's request. And after reconsideration of my prior order, that order is to stand. The defendant's brief and appendix remain due on or before November 18, 2019. (Blake, J.). *Notice.
